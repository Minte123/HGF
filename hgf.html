The Solomonic Paradox

The wiser you appear,
the less you ask for help.

High-performing individuals use their intelligence to reason clearly about others' pain — then apply that same intelligence to minimize and hide their own. The more capable you appear, the higher the stakes of being seen as struggling.

"I've known you for 20 years and never saw a single sign. He said — 'That's because I did everything I could to make sure no one knew.'"

— Neurosurgeon on the death of a colleague, AANS Annual Meeting, 2024

The Habesha Dimension

Between two worlds,
no language for this pain.

For Ethiopian and Eritrean communities — in the U.S. and at home — mental health is not a clinical gap. It is a cultural silence with structural roots. In Habesha culture, emotional suffering is coded as spiritual failure or family shame. The most capable individuals suffer the most invisibly.

A 2025 meta-analysis found 36% of Ethiopian youth are depressed — above WHO benchmarks. A separate 2025 study established that 12.38% have attempted suicide — in a country with fewer than 100 psychiatrists for 130 million people and no national prevention system.

For diaspora Ethiopians and Eritreans in the U.S., this crisis travels. Ethnic conflict, displacement, and grief compound identity fracture — while no existing U.S. mental health system was designed for this intersection.

A Physician Across Continents — The Full Story

Dr. Frew H. Gebreab graduated from Jimma University College of Public Health and Medical Sciences in 1995 — one of Ethiopia's foremost medical institutions. His first posting after graduation was as a physician at a remotely located public health center in rural Ethiopia, practicing as what would be called a general practitioner in the U.S. context: a single doctor serving an entire community, with scarce supplies, no specialist backup, and patients traveling hours to reach care. In his own words, those years "were rewarding and taught me a lot about humility and compassion for the sick."

But Dr. Gebreab was never only a clinician. Before leaving Ethiopia, he took on a different kind of service — becoming the Director of an international nonprofit that delivered clean water, immunization programs, and prenatal care to underserved communities. This was medicine understood at the systems level: not just treating the patient in front of you, but building the conditions in which far fewer people need treatment at all. It was his first act of institutional creation — and a direct ancestor of everything HGF has become.

In 1999, he immigrated to the United States, carrying both formations with him. He completed his internal medicine residency at New York-Presbyterian Brooklyn Methodist Hospital, then pursued a geriatrics fellowship at University Hospitals Cleveland Medical Center, Case Western Reserve University — gaining the added perspective, as he has described it, of "dealing with the social, medical and end-of-life issues of critically sick elderly patients." He returned to New York for a pulmonary medicine fellowship at New York-Presbyterian Brooklyn Methodist Hospital, followed by a critical care fellowship at Memorial Sloan Kettering Cancer Center — one of the most demanding clinical training environments in the world. He later added sleep medicine to his specialties.

In 2007, he joined Kaiser Permanente in Southern California, where he has practiced at the Fontana Medical Center ever since — board-certified in Critical Care Medicine, Pulmonary Disease, and Internal Medicine, fluent in English and Amharic, and building a clinical reputation over more than two decades as a physician who listens, explains, and stays present with the most seriously ill.

The ICU, and What It Cost

For years, Dr. Gebreab practiced as a dedicated, compassionate intensivist — a physician whose entire working life was organized around keeping the most critically ill patients alive in the most demanding environment medicine offers. The ICU demands everything: irreversible decisions under time pressure, repeated loss, sustained exposure to suffering, and the professional expectation that none of this will show.

He gave everything to it. And over years of intensive practice, something accumulated beneath the surface of that functioning — something the professional culture he worked inside was precisely structured to prevent him from naming, and that his Habesha cultural inheritance provided no language for either.

In 2018, Dr. Gebreab was diagnosed with PTSD. The diagnosis did not come quickly. It took time — during which the condition had already begun to affect those around him in ways that neither he nor they could fully understand. The delay is not unusual. It is typical. It is exactly what happens when a person is trained to suppress distress as a professional virtue, and when the cultural surround offers shame instead of support. The Solomonic Paradox does not produce a sudden collapse. It produces a slow, invisible erosion — with a delayed diagnosis and a circle of impact that extends well beyond the individual carrying it.

Recovery — and a First Act of Building

He recovered. And in the space that recovery opened, he did not simply return to what he had been doing. He started building.

Dr. Gebreab founded a self-funded family nonprofit — an early expression of the same impulse that would eventually become HGF: the conviction that the most meaningful response to a gap is not to document it, but to fill it. In 2018, the work of that organization included two acts of direct, community-centered giving that defined its character:

First, a cataract surgery campaign that restored sight to 50 elderly blind individuals — people who had been living without vision, for whom the intervention was not incremental but transformational. Fifty people who could see again.

Second, Dr. Gebreab and his family provided material and monetary support to residents of Mekedonia Humanitarian Association — one of Ethiopia's most important humanitarian institutions, based in Addis Ababa, which shelters and cares for elderly, mentally ill, physically disabled, and destitute individuals who have no one else. The family's contribution brought material goods and direct financial support to the people living in Mekedonia's care.

These two acts — together — established something Dr. Gebreab has carried into every subsequent initiative: that direct, human-scale giving, delivered with cultural knowledge and personal commitment, reaches people that large systems cannot. HGF is that same impulse, built to institutional scale.

The Convergence That Created HGF

After his initial recovery, the PTSD recurred. A second episode — not uncommon in a condition this complex, especially when the underlying stressors have not been structurally changed. And in that recurrence, something shifted in how Dr. Gebreab saw the world around him.

He began to notice patterns he had not been able to see before — or had seen but not yet assembled into a whole:

The effect of COVID-19 on mental health — not just the acute crisis of the pandemic, but the sustained, unnamed psychological toll it levied on healthcare workers, on communities, on young people who lost formative years to isolation and grief, and on a diaspora population already carrying compounded trauma.

Social media and the performance of wellbeing — platforms that amplified the pressure to appear fine, to project success, to perform wellness, while making the gap between public appearance and private experience wider and more unbridgeable than ever.

Changing sociopolitical conditions — the fracturing of trust, the rise of ethnic nationalism, the destabilization of communities that had constructed identity around assumptions that no longer held.

Increasing ethnic violence in Ethiopia — the Tigray conflict and ongoing inter-ethnic violence that added mass trauma to a diaspora population already navigating displacement, grief, and divided loyalty. Communities that had built lives in the United States were watching their home country burn, with no framework for processing that grief in either cultural context.

And most urgently: his personal observation of increasing suicide among high-performing young individuals in the Habesha community — in the United States and back in Ethiopia. Not people in visible crisis. People who were succeeding, achieving, functioning. Who no one saw coming. Whose deaths left those around them with the same devastating sentence: "I had no idea."

At the same time, he was watching close colleagues — physicians, healthcare workers — burn out and deteriorate without recognizing what was happening to them. Not dramatic collapse. Slow erosion. Depression and burnout presenting as irritability, cynicism, withdrawal — symptoms that the medical culture around them coded as personality, not pathology.

These observations converged into a single recognition: there was no organization designed for exactly this intersection. No entity that addressed the mental health of high-performing individuals within Habesha cultural context, physician wellness within a non-punitive framework, and the structural absence of prevention systems in both the U.S. diaspora and Ethiopia — simultaneously.

So he built one. The Harasemay Global Foundation.

Personal Disclosure — PTSD Survivor · 2018

Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder in a physician is not what most people imagine. It does not look like collapse. It does not interrupt function. In Dr. Gebreab's case — as in the cases of most high-functioning individuals with PTSD — it was invisible by design. The same competence that made him effective clinically became the mechanism that concealed his distress. He functioned. He performed. He was present for his patients. And underneath that performance was a crisis the systems around him were structurally unable to address.

What the general public does not yet fully appreciate is that PTSD is not a disorder of weakness. It is a normal neurological response to sustained exposure to abnormal stress — and the ICU provides that stress in concentrated, repeated doses. Every death that can't be undone. Every family notified. Every decision made under pressure with incomplete information. Every colleague who is also suffering and also not saying so. This accumulates. And in a culture that treats any sign of psychological distress as a liability — professionally, legally, reputationally — it accumulates in silence.

For Dr. Gebreab, the silence was also cultural. As a Habesha physician, he carried the additional weight of a community tradition in which emotional suffering is not a clinical condition to be treated but a spiritual or personal failure to be concealed. The professional culture of medicine said: don't disclose, it will cost you. The Habesha cultural inheritance said: don't disclose, it is shame. Two powerful suppression systems operating simultaneously — producing the precise outcome both were designed to prevent.

He sought help. He recovered. He came through. And in coming through, he faced a choice that every survivor eventually faces: whether to keep the experience private, or to make it useful. He chose to make it useful.

Every program HGF runs — every piece of policy advocacy, every structural safeguard, every culturally embedded intervention — is shaped by the specific knowledge that comes from having been that person: capable, credentialed, visibly functional, and in a crisis that the surrounding systems were not built to see. The Solomonic Paradox is not a theory Dr. Gebreab developed by observing others. It is a description of his own experience.

He discloses this publicly because visibility is itself a clinical intervention. When a physician — trained at the highest levels, practiced across continents, dedicated to others' survival — names PTSD as part of his own story, it changes what feels possible for every person who reads it and recognizes themselves. That recognition — that someone capable and credible has been exactly where you are — is sometimes the first thing that makes asking for help feel survivable rather than catastrophic.

His Message

"I graduated from Jimma University in Ethiopia in 1995. I worked as a rural general practitioner in a remote public health center — a single physician for an entire community. Then I became the director of an international nonprofit delivering clean water, immunization, and prenatal care to communities that had no one else. I came to the United States in 1999. I did residency, then geriatrics at Case Western, then pulmonary and critical care at Sloan Kettering — some of the most demanding training environments in medicine. I joined Kaiser Permanente in 2007 and worked for years as a dedicated, compassionate intensivist. And in 2018 I was diagnosed with PTSD. Not because I was weak. Because I was human, in a profession that asks you to absorb everything and say nothing.

I know what it is to function at a high level while something is quietly destroying you from the inside. I know what it costs the people around you when the diagnosis comes late — and it always comes late, because the systems around physicians and the cultures around Habesha people are both structured to make sure it does. I recovered. Then it came back. And in the recurrence, I started seeing what I had not been able to see before: the same silence in my colleagues, the same erosion in my community, young people in the diaspora dying by suicide with no one who saw it coming, an entire generation of high performers carrying a crisis that has no name in the cultures they come from.

I built HGF because I could see the gap, and I had the experience, the community knowledge, and the clinical understanding to do something about it. I am building the system I needed — and the one my colleagues needed, and the one fifty young people in the Habesha community needed before it was too late. I am not doing it from a research paper or a policy brief. I am doing it from memory. And I am doing it so the next person who finds themselves in that space does not have to build their own way out."

— Frew H. Gebreab, MD · Founder & President, Harasemay Global Foundation · California, USA

U.S. College Students Mental Health · Suicidal Ideation

Healthy Minds Study: Annual Report 2023–24 and 2024–25
Healthy Minds Network. University of Michigan. 2024–2025.

Physicians & Healthcare Workers Burnout · Suicide · PTSD

Medscape
Medscape Physician Suicide Report 2023
Medscape. Published 2023.
~400 U.S. physicians die by suicide annually; 9% have considered it; female physician risk 94% higher than general female population
medscape.com ↗
Physicians Foundation
Survey of America's Physicians 2023
Physicians Foundation. Published 2023.
78% of physicians agree there is stigma; only 19% have sought mental health treatment
physiciansfoundation.org ↗

Ethiopia & East Africa Youth Suicide · Depression

Ferede AT, Yeshaw Y, Dagnew B, et al.
Prevalence and associated factors of suicidal attempt among adolescents and youth in Ethiopia: A systematic review and meta-analysis
BMC Psychiatry. 2025;25:160. doi:10.1186/s12888-025-06604-9
12.38% suicide attempt rate (95% CI 8.72–16.05%); girls 2.3×; family history 5.7×; poor social support 3.4×; poor academics 3.2×

Child and Adolescent Psychiatry and Mental Health. 2025;19:115.
